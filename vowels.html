    Vocabulary defined


  • Voicing
    As we've already seen, voicing is the vibration of the vocal chords and an important part of how consonants are described. This doesn't work woith the vowel sounds.
    Make the /i/ sound. It's voiced, right? Now, do the same with /u/. It's voiced too. In English, all vowels are voiced. In fact, most vowels are voiced. Voiceless vowels are uncommon.

  • Tongue placement
    We can't use manner and place of arituclation for vowels. Make an /f/. You feel how you lower lip curls back to your teeth, right? Now make a /u/ sound. Your tongue is pulled back in your mouth, but it's not touching anything. So place of articulation is out the window. Manner of articulation is, too, because it describes how we restrict the airflow to make sounds. Vowels don't restrict airflow, either. So, we use tongue placement.

    We divide the mouth in quadrants: front, mid, and back and high, mid and low. Look at this chart:



    So, when we describe a vowel, we use tongue placement and the tense v. lax distinction. (see below), so the /i/ sound is a high, front vowel.

  • Diphthongs and Triphthongs
    Diphthongs are essentailly two vowel sounds shoved together. English has several, /eI/, /oƱ/, /aI/, /aƱ/, /ɔI/ and many of the rhotic vowels. When discussing tongue placement, I pulled out /aI, aƱ, ɔI/ because the tongue moves when you make those sounds.
    Make the /eI/ sound. Feel houw your tongue doesn't really move? Now make the /aƱ/ sound. Feel how your mouth moves? That makes describing them much more complicated. So, for the purposes of our classes, we'll just call /aI, aƱ, ɔI/ "Diphthongs". Just remember that they aren't the only siphthongs we have.
    Tripthongs are three vowel sounds smashed together. Most dialects of English don't have them. However, the English spoken in Australia and New Zealand does./blockquote>

  • Rhotic Vowels
    The r sound does some interesting things. When it starts a word or syllable, like in "run", or part of a consonant cluster, like "brush", it's a consonant. When it ends a syllable or a word, like "hear", or just follows a vowel, like "start", it is a vowel sounds. Sometimes, it stands on its own, like in "heard", sometimes it combines with another vowel sound to make a rhotic diphthong, like in "tear".

  • Lip rounding
    Some vowels require lip rounding. Make the /u/ sound. Feel how your lips round? That's how it works. In English, lip-rounding only occurs with back vowels.
    Many languages, like French and German, have front, rounded vowels.

  • Tense v. lax
    Along with tongue placement, this is one of the contrasts used to describe vowels in English. Tense vowels usually involve effort to pronounce than lax vowels. MAke an /i/ sound. Do you feel how your jaw is moving forward and your mouth is opening up like your smiling? Now make an /I/ sound. Your tongue is in the same area as when you makie an /i/ but your jaw is moving really, and your mouth isn;t opening as much. That makes it lax.

    So, these vowels are described this way: /i/: high, front, tense vowel while /I/ is a high, front, lax vowel.

    The Vowels in Detail

  • /i/
    Sample words: meet eternal, fee
    Description: High, front, tense

  • /I/
    Sample words: ill, tip, rhythm
    Description: High, front, lax

  • /eI/
    Sample words: Ale, plate, grey
    Description: Mid, front, tense

  • /ɛ/
    Sample words: Fred, net, speck
    Description: Mid, front, lax

  • /æ/
    Sample words: Canada, banana, plant
    Description: Low, front, lax

  • /u/
    Sample words: usual, Hugh, blue
    Description: High, back tense

  • /Ʊ/
    Sample words: wood, should, cook
    Description: High, back, lax

  • /oƱ/
    Sample words: oath, poke, flow
    Description: Mid, back, tense

  • /ɔ/
    Sample words: coffee, fraught, bought
    Description: Mid, back, lax
    In many North American English dialects, this sound has merged with /a/ (see below).

  • /ə/
    Sample words: before, nation, alone
    Description: Mid, mid, lax
    This is the schwa, an unstressed vowel. It is very short, and it said very quickly.

  • /ʌ/
    Sample words: mug, pluck, love
    Description: Mid, mid, lax
    This vowel, the carat, is closely related to the schwa. It's basically the schwa but longer. IT's actually more complicated than that. but this explanation will work for now.

  • /ɚ/
    Sample words: perfection, actor, seperate (when it's a verb)
    Description: Mid, mid, lax
    This is the rhotic version of the schwa.

  • /ɝ/
    Sample words: colonel, person, unfurl
    Description: Mid, mid, lax
    This is the rhotic version of the carat.

  • /a/
    Sample words: plot, dot, lot
    Description: Low, back, tense
    In many North American dialects, /ɔ/ is merging with this sound. Also, sometimes, this is called a low, mid vowel.

  • /aI/
    Sample words: island, kite, fly
    Description: Diphthong

  • /aƱ/
    Sample words: owl, house, plow
    Description: Diphthong

  • /ɔI/
    Sample words: oil, avoid, toy
    Description: Diphthong

  • /Iɚ/
    Sample words: near, jeer, cheer
    Description: high, front, rhotic

  • /ɛɚ/
    Sample words: care, share, flair
    Description: Rhotic

  • /aɚ/
    Sample words: arch, tar, alarm
    Description: Rhotic

  • /Ʊɚ/
    Sample words: cure, sure, lure
    Description: Rhotic

  • /ɔɚ/
    Sample words: order, fore, pour
    Description: Rhotic

  • /aIɚ/
    Sample words: hire, admire, liar
    Description: Rhotic

  • /aƱɚ/
    Sample words: Flour, shower, cower
    Description: Rhotic
