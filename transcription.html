Basics of Transcription

Transcription is has many meanings, and is a word used in many fields. For our purposes, transcription is the process of taking a word in English and rewriting it in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) and vice versa.


So, for example, take the word "flute". When it's transcribed into IPA, it looks like this: /flut/.

Here, there isn't much difference between English and IPA. This isn't always the case. Take the work "shrink", which looks like /ʃrIŋk/ when transcribed. You need to be careful. Spelling isn't always your friend.

    Some tips

  • English loves silent letters. So just because a word has six letters, that doesn't mean that the IPA transciption will. Take the word "knight". In IPA, it's /naIt/.


  • English also uses combinations of letters to represent a single sound, like "sh", "th", and "tch".


  • In English spelling, letters can be pronounced different ways. Take the letter "x". It can be pronounced three different ways.

    1. When "x" starts a word, "x" is usually pronounced /z/, like in Xylophone: /zaIləfoƱn/.


    2. When "x" is surrounded by voiced sounds, it represents two voiced sounds. /gz/, like in "exit": /ɛgzIt/.


    3. Everywhere else, "x" represents two voiceless sounds: /ks/, like in tax: /tæks/.


  • "Qu" is another good example. This consonant cluster has two different pronounciations:

    1. It's usually pronounced /kw/, as in quick: /kwIk/


    2. Sometimes, it's pronounced /k/, as in pique: /pik/
  • Some words have multiple pronunciations.

    1. Sometimes, the different pronunciations don't change the meaning of the word, like "either". In IPA, it's /iðɚ/ or /aIðɚ/.


    2. Sometimes, the meaning does change, as in "record".

      When the stress is on the first syllable, it's /rɛkɚd/. This is the noun. When it falls on the second syllable, it's pronounced /rIkɔɚd/ and it's a verb.

      English loves doing this. We have many words that do this.
