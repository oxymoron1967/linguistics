  • Nouns

    Nouns represent persons, places, things, or ideas. Examples include "Bill", "mountain", "cup", and "thought".



  • Verbs

    Verbs are action words. Examples include "think", "went", and "breathe". Auxiliary verbs indicate tense and mode, while the main verb is usually a participle. Examples of helping verbs include "should", "can", and "may".





  • Adjectives

    Adjectives describe nouns. Examples include "big", "crisp", and "nice".





  • Adverbs

    Adverbs describe verbs, adjectives, and other adverbs. They usually end in -ly. Examples include "quickly", "lovely", and "quietly".





  • Articles

    Articles modify nouns. English has three: the definite article "the", and the indefinite articles "a" and "an".

    Articles are sometimes classified with other parts of speech. See the complications entry below.

  • Conjunctions

    Conjunctions join words or phrases together. Examples include "and", "although", and "if".





  • Prepositions

    Prepositions are small words that usually describe where, when, why or how something takes place. They are usually followed by a noun phrase. These are called prepositional phrases. Examples include "to", "at", and "in".





  • Pronouns

    Pronouns are small words that replace nouns that have previously been referred to. There are several categories of pronouns including:

    • Personal pronouns. Examples include "I", "you", and "they"
    • Possessive pronouns. Examples include "your", "his", and "her"
    • Demonstrative pronouns. Examples include "this", "that", "these", and "those".
    • Question words. Well, sometimes, see complications below. Examples inclide "who", "what", and "when".




  • Interjections

    Interjections are short words or phrases that express reactions. Examples include "wow", "ouch", and "hey".

  • Complications

    • Compound words

      English loves to make new words by joining words together. Sometimes, these can complicate matters:

      • Compound Nouns

        Usually, compound nouns are written as one word, such as "airport" or "wheelchair", or they're attached with a hyphen, like "e-mail". Sometimes, though, compound nouns are still written as separate words, like White House or United States. So, even though White House looks like an adjective and a noun, in terms of word stress, rhythm, and intonation, it's considered one unit.

        Look at these sentences:

        1. Dave toured the White House when he visited D.C.


        2. The white house on the corner burned down last week.


        There is a slight but noticeable difference in pronunciation of "White House" in the first sentence and "white house" in the second.


      • Compound verbs

        English likes to pair verbs with prepositions. Prepositions usually are followed by noun phrases, but in this format they don't always. Look at these sentences:

        1. I woke up early this morning.
        2. Money is a little tight right now, but I'm getting by.


        Note that neither "up" nor "by" have a noun phrase following them.


    • *sigh* Articles are messy.

      1. Sometimes, the word "some" is added to the articles as a plural indefinite article.


      2. Usually, they are listed in their own separate category.


      3. Sometimes, articles are lumped in with adjectives, because they do modify nouns and noun phrases.


      4. Sometimes, they are thrown into a category called determiners, which includes articles, demonstrative pronouns, questions words, and possessive pronouns. These words frequently show deixis.




    • Words that move from category to category

      • Depending on context, many words can change categoies. Sometimes, the pronunciation of the word changes, such as the following:

        • "Record": When the stress is on the first syllable, it's a noun; when it's on the second syllable, it's a verb. English has many words like this.


        • "Invalid": When the stress is on the first syllable, it's a noun; when it's on the second syllable, it's an adjective.


      • But, frequently, pronunciation isn't much of a help.

        • "Who" is either a question word, as in "Who just came in?", but when it's not asking a question, it's a conjunction, as in "Mike is the man who was fired."


        • "So" is either an adverb, as in "The blizzard was so brutally cold", or it's a conjunction, as in "Alan finished work quickly so he could start his vacation early."


      • Auxiliary verbs

        • Usually, this is a straighforward construction, like this: "I should finish my homework."


        • However, you can stack auxiliary verbs, so you get phrases like this: "Dan might have been going to the movies".


        • Also, some verbs can be auxiliary verbs in some constructions but be the main verbs in others. Look at the foloowing sentences:

          1. I have already fixed the problem.


          2. I have the book with me.


          In the first sentence, "have" is an auxiliary verb. In the second sentence, it is a main verb.
      • And then we have the word "that".

        • "That" can be a conjunction, as in "Ann saw that the restaurant was closed" or a demonstrative pronoun as in "Could you hand me that book?".


        • This leads to constructions like this (From the Gettysburg Address): "We have come to dedicate a portion of that field, as a final resting place for those who here gave their lives that that nation might live.

          It might feel wrong to have those two "that"s next to one another, but it is absolutely correct.
